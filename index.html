How I Think & Work

Work philosophy &
guiding principles

Mathematical Logic in Everyday Problems

I apply the same logic I use in proofs and models to everyday decisions. Define the problem clearly. Identify the variables. Remove assumptions. Find the elegant solution. Whether it's planning a week, debugging a pipeline, or making a difficult decision under uncertainty — I try to think like a mathematician first.

This means I'm comfortable sitting with problems I don't immediately understand. I've learned that discomfort at the beginning of a hard problem is not a signal to stop — it's a signal you're working on something real.

Meditation & Focus Under Pressure

I practice meditation to stay sharp under tight deadlines. When everything is urgent, nothing is clear. Meditation is how I recover my ability to prioritize, think structurally, and separate noise from signal — especially in high-pressure deliveries.

It's not mystical — it's a mental practice for clarity. The same way I warm up before training, I use stillness before a complex analytical task. It works.

My Work Principles

01Define the problem before touching the data. Experts spend 40% of their time understanding what's actually being asked. I start every project by writing the business question in one sentence — if I can't, I'm not ready to open a notebook yet.
02Communicate results, not processes. Senior analysts present conclusions with evidence, not methodology walls. I always ask: what does this decision-maker need to know, and what's the cleanest way to show it?
03Think in systems, not just solutions. A fix that creates three downstream problems isn't a fix — it's debt. Before writing a single line of code I map the full data flow: where it comes from, what breaks if it changes, and what depends on it. Systems thinking is what separates analysts who patch problems from engineers who prevent them.
04Validate assumptions ruthlessly. Experts know that the most expensive mistakes come from unchallenged assumptions baked into the data model. I build in sanity checks at every layer — not just at the end.
05Make the invisible visible. The most valuable insight is never in the summary statistic — it's in what the summary stat is hiding. I go one layer deeper than the brief asks for, because that's where the real decision lives. That extra layer has changed the conclusion of every serious project I've worked on.

My Principles of Life

01Excellence. I don't aim for good enough. Whatever I work on — a model, a presentation, a conversation — I try to make it genuinely excellent. Not perfectionism, but craftsmanship.
02Balance. High performance requires recovery. I train, spend time in nature, disconnect from screens, and protect sleep — not because I'm told to, but because I've seen what happens to my thinking when I don't.
03Perseverance. The projects that taught me the most were the ones where I wanted to quit halfway. I've learned to distinguish between productive struggle — which means keep going — and unproductive friction — which means change strategy.
04Patience. Good things — skills, relationships, careers — compound slowly. I've stopped optimizing for fast and started optimizing for deep. Patience isn't passivity; it's trusting the process while staying in motion.
05Innovation. I'm not interested in doing things the way they've always been done. I ask "why does this work this way?" about everything — processes, assumptions, tools. The best solutions rarely come from inside the existing framework.

Career · Business Thinking · Germany
What Living in Germany Taught Me About Data Science — and Why Most Data Scientists Have a Dangerous Blind Spot
Most Data Scientists have the same blind spot — they're excellent at models and code, but they've never sat in a room where a P&L is being discussed, where a process owner explains why a workflow exists, or where a product decision gets made without a single line of SQL. That isolation is expensive. You build technically sound solutions to the wrong problems.
Going to CBS International Business School in Germany fixed that for me. I wasn't in an engineering classroom — I was studying business models, strategy, and how organizations actually operate. That forced me to develop a completely different mental model: before asking "what does the data say," I started asking "what decision is this data supposed to support, and who's making it?"
The result was a specific skill combination that's genuinely rare at my level: I can look at a business process and immediately understand its structure — who the stakeholders are, what the incentive is, where the friction is — and then translate that into a data architecture that actually serves it. Business context shapes what data you collect. Data structure determines what decisions you can make. Most people have one or the other. I came back from Germany with both.
SmartShelf was the proof of concept. I didn't just build a pricing model — I mapped the operational workflow, identified where the financial loss was happening, built the business case, and presented it to stakeholders in terms they could act on. That only happens when you understand the business as well as you understand the data.

AI · Labor · Society
The Jobs AI Will Actually Kill — and the One Skill That Makes You Untouchable
Everyone talks about AI replacing jobs. Almost nobody talks about which specific jobs, at which specific layer, and why. After spending a semester in Germany studying business models alongside AI systems, I stopped asking "will AI take my job?" and started asking a much more useful question: which part of my job can a model do better than me — and what does that leave?
The answer isn't comforting. Routine analysis, templated reporting, data cleaning at scale — these are already being automated faster than most analytics teams are willing to admit. What isn't being automated is judgment under uncertainty: knowing which question to ask when the data doesn't tell you, translating a business crisis into a data problem, deciding what not to measure. That's the gap. And it's only valuable if you can sit on both sides of it — technical enough to build the model, business-literate enough to know when not to trust it.
My thesis: the data professionals who will thrive in the next decade aren't the ones who know the most tools. They're the ones who understand the business well enough to know which tool matters — and have the communication skills to make a non-technical decision-maker act on the output.
If AI can run the analysis, what exactly are you being paid for?

"The analyst who understands the business will always outperform the one who only understands the data."

Why business context is the only moat left in data science — and how to build it deliberately.

Banking · Analytics · Society
What a 93% Acceptance Rate Hides — and Who It Harms
A 93.1% offer acceptance rate sounds like a success story. It isn't. When you work with 50,000+ real banking records across 8 relational tables — as I did in the Qlik project at Tec de Monterrey with an industry partner — that headline number starts to collapse the moment you disaggregate it.
The 6.9% rejection rate wasn't random. It was concentrated almost entirely in one segment: Premium clients being evaluated for Top-Up products. These are clients the bank already has a relationship with — clients with the highest lifetime value — and the system was rejecting them at a disproportionate rate for a single criterion they didn't meet. Credit Policies and Risk Score were the top reasons across the full population, but for Premium clients specifically, the pattern was stark: one structural bottleneck was silently destroying conversion in the bank's most valuable segment.
This is what aggregate KPIs hide. A 93% rate looks like a well-oiled machine. But inside it, there's a systematic exclusion pattern that, left unaddressed, slowly erodes the relationship with the clients who matter most. The proposal I built — a pre-approval system based on client history — wasn't about increasing the acceptance rate. It was about eliminating friction for clients who had already earned trust and were being failed by a process that wasn't built with them in mind.
The broader lesson: high-level metrics are management comfort food. They feel good, they're easy to report, and they actively prevent you from seeing the problem. Real analytics starts when you stop celebrating the 93% and start asking who lives inside the 7% — and why.
